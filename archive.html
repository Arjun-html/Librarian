Signal Processing

Oppenheim & Willsky's Signals Classic: Fourier Analysis, Systems Theory, and the Mathematics of Everything That Oscillates

The definitive treatment of continuous and discrete signals — worked through for a university course of the same name, and still consulted regularly since
By Alan V. Oppenheim & Alan S. Willsky

Few textbooks endure the passage from coursework companion to permanent reference so cleanly as Oppenheim and Willsky's Signals and Systems. The Fourier transform, the Laplace transform, convolution, and the discrete-time equivalents of each receive a treatment thorough enough to form the mathematical foundation for a career in communications, control, or digital signal processing. The book was assigned for a university course of the same name; it earned a place on the shelf well beyond the semester's end. Already there are thoughts about building something that puts these tools to work — a good sign that the material was absorbed rather than merely passed.

Quantitative Finance

Zuckerman's Simons Biography: Inspiration in the First Half, Sobering Reckoning in the Second

Renaissance Technologies laid bare — Jim Simons's journey from pure mathematics to the most profitable trading operation in history, and what extreme wealth subsequently wrought among those around him
By Gregory Zuckerman

Gregory Zuckerman's biography of Jim Simons divides neatly into two very different books. The first chronicles the unlikely journey of a world-class mathematician who decided, in middle age, to apply rigorous quantitative methods to financial markets — and succeeded beyond any reasonable expectation, building the Medallion Fund into the most profitable trading operation in history. The second half traces what extreme wealth and power did to the people around him. The inspiration of the first half is genuine; so is the horror of watching that same success fund political projects of a rather different character. The book's final lesson is the importance of remaining an active, critical thinker regardless of the company one keeps.

Philosophy & Fiction

Coelho's Alchemist: The Book That Warms on Every Return — and Gave Goosebumps on the First Reading

A shepherd's journey in search of treasure becomes a meditation on listening to one's heart — brief, luminous, and unexpectedly durable across many rereads
By Paulo Coelho

Paulo Coelho's The Alchemist is the rare book that does not diminish on return visits. A shepherd boy, a journey, an alchemist, and a treasure that turns out to mean something rather different from what was expected — the outline sounds modest, but Coelho's execution produces something that has proven genuinely affecting across millions of readers and multiple rereads. The book warms something specific. It gave goosebumps on first reading. Returning to it still produces that same sensation, undimmed. Some books earn their reputation honestly, without any need of explanation or defence.
